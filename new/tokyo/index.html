Make It So Camp Tokyo

You cannot delegate what you cannot articulate.

Two days at Crypto Café Tokyo for practitioners ready to turn tacit expertise into clear instructions, reusable methods, and a rough working thing they can build with others and demo back to the room.

  • 24–25 August 2026
  • Crypto Café Tokyo
  • With Chiba Institute of Technology
Academic partner

Before the camp

Arrive with the room already visible.

The work starts before everyone sits down together. A short AI interview gives the facilitators a view of what participants are wrestling with, so Day 1 begins from real patterns rather than generic assumptions.

Pre-event interviews through Boswell

Each participant does a 15–20 minute AI interview instead of a written application. It probes AI experience, workflow, use cases, and tools. The responses cluster into a live heat map of what is in the room, shown on Day 1.

Bring-your-own prerequisites

Participants bring their own tokens, subscriptions, laptop, and some of their current ways of working. Day 1 runs on paper, with laptops closed until the Day 2 build.

Day 1

Implant the Thinking

Day 1 moves through the four Shifts. Every block ends hands-on, on paper, and seeds what participants build on Day 2.

01

Welcome + Frame

The positioning shift: purposeful, not faster.

Input
Arrive, settle, and see the Boswell heat map. The room starts with its own patterns in view.
Apply
Trace a technology two or three hops out, then name one task you would merely accelerate and one thing you could newly build.
Capture
Your candidate problem, framed as an ambition rather than a first-hop task.
02

Shift 2 · Methods You Can't See

The articulation shift: the bottleneck is articulation, not the tech.

Input
Your method is invisible until you can teach it to a machine.
Apply
Run a literal-instructions exercise, then map delegation for your real problem with a partner probing for gaps.
Capture
Your two-sentence method or decision logic.
03

Shift 3 · Write It Down, It Compounds

The reuse shift: documented once, reused all day.

Input
A head-start document is what lets the next build begin from shared context.
Apply
Draft a head-start for your Day 2 problem, then test whether a fresh pair of eyes would understand it.
Capture
The head-start document itself.
04

Shift 4 · Explicit = Shareable

The collaboration shift: if it is explicit, someone else can run it.

Input
Explicit methods can be challenged, improved, and shared.
Apply
Compare head-starts in your heat-map cluster and look for overlaps, contradictions, and useful matches.
Capture
A shared starting protocol, plus who you may want to build with.

Day 2

Look at What It Produced

The second day turns the Day 1 captures into collaborative build work. Participants team up or work solo, build from the methods they made explicit, critique each other, and demo to each other in rounds before an early finish.

Build together

Groups and solo builders use the Day 1 problem and head-start document to build toward a clear demo target. The emphasis is on making the method usable with other people, while facilitators circulate through the room during the build blocks.

Demo to each other

Rather than one long sequence of presentations, groups of about four demo to each other in rounds. The format gives high exposure to other work and lets participants see how other teams translated explicit methods into artifacts.

Wrap + early finish

A short synthesis closes the camp around 15:00 so participants can travel home with the method and artifact still fresh.
